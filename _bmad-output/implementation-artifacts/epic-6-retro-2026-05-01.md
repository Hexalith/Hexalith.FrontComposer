## What Went Well

- The gradient is now concrete, not conceptual. Developers have a path from metadata-only customization through full replacement.
- The team kept the public API typed wherever it mattered: projection templates, slot overrides, view overrides, render contexts, and diagnostic contracts.
- Code review was effective. Review passes found duplicate behavior, stale context risks, render-context fallback implications, localization gaps, accessibility analyzer scope gaps, and diagnostic panel weaknesses before the retrospective.
- Regression discipline improved across the epic. Snapshot rebaselines were tied to explicit behavior changes, and final validation numbers rose as boundary and catalog tests were added.
- The Counter sample became a useful executable narrative for the customization gradient.

## Challenges

- Scope pressure was high. Story 6-5 and Story 6-6 repeatedly attracted analyzer, accessibility, overlay, starter, runtime boundary, and documentation concerns.
- Several acceptance criteria were stronger than the implemented evidence. The team handled this honestly by amending Story 6-6 ACs and cataloging deferred work instead of pretending everything was closed.
- Some Story 6-3 follow-ups were originally assigned to Story 6-6 but remained deferred or moved to Epic 7, Epic 9, or Epic 10 after review clarified ownership.
- Dev-mode and diagnostics touched many layers at once: Contracts, SourceTools, Shell, generated output, samples, resources, JavaScript interop, and tests.
- The sprint status indicates Epic 7 work is already underway, so handoff risks need to be handled in-flight rather than before kickoff.

## Key Lessons

1. Typed descriptors and descriptor-only registries are the right backbone for customization.
2. The customization surface needs explicit ownership boundaries. Level 2, Level 3, Level 4, dev mode, diagnostics, docs, and visual/accessibility specimen work should not absorb each other by default.
3. Runtime preview ergonomics and tenant isolation can conflict. The Story 6-3 sentinel `RenderContext` fallback is useful for previews, but Epic 7-2 must decide where tenant-aware render context becomes fail-closed.
4. Diagnostic IDs are now product surface. The catalog, docs-link shape, message sections, and release notes must remain governed.
5. "Done" needs two layers: story AC closure and deferred-work ownership. Epic 6 improved both, but the deferred-work tail is now large enough to require active tracking.

## Significant Discovery

Epic 6 does not fundamentally invalidate Epic 7's plan, but it adds one important requirement to Story 7-2:

Customization hosts now have their own typed contexts and preview fallback behavior. Tenant isolation cannot be treated only as command/query plumbing; it must also be considered at render-context boundaries for templates, slots, view replacements, dev-mode diagnostics, and runtime fault panels.

Epic update required: No broad rewrite. Add explicit Story 7-2 acceptance/checklist coverage for customization render context and tenant-safe diagnostics.

## Commitments

- Keep the customization gradient stable as a typed contract surface.
- Treat deferred work as owned product backlog, not hidden technical debt.
- Keep diagnostics teachable, localized where user-visible, and safe by default.
- Do not let dev-mode convenience leak into production behavior.
- Apply Epic 6 lessons directly to Epic 7 tenant, auth, and authorization boundaries.
