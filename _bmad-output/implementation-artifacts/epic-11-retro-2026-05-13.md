## Successes

1. The ledger became audit-capable. Story 11.1 created deterministic row IDs and made the current state machine explicit enough to inspect.
2. Review findings drove better implementation. Stories 11.2, 11.5, and 11.7 all record fresh review patches that materially improved validation, evidence quality, and row-scoped decisions.
3. Validation stayed close to the touched surface. Each story used focused tests first, then broader release/main-lane checks where appropriate.
4. Release evidence got safer. Redaction scans, forbidden-token checks, root-level submodule governance, and evidence-before-side-effect ordering show the team internalized the release-trust lessons from Epic 10.
5. Scope boundaries were mostly disciplined. Stories split or accepted adjacent work rather than silently turning one hardening bucket into a hidden multi-epic rewrite.

## Challenges

1. Story-complete does not equal release-complete. Several rows are accepted constraints or split follow-ups, and at least one release-blocking split remains around provider-backed pending-command status.
2. The ledger summary and current markers can drift. Story 11.5 says it closed its review decisions, but direct inspection still finds current `Reconciliation: ... Owner: Story 11.5` markers in the ledger.
3. Evidence quality varies by story. Story 11.6 added a strong row-evidence matrix, but its code review also noted boilerplate acceptance rationales that were not distinct enough per row.
4. Some safeguards are representative, not exhaustive. Accessibility, localization, visual, RTL, release credential, provider-backed EventStore, and broad diagnostic polish remain bounded by explicit acceptance or split decisions.
5. Sprint status still needs bookkeeping policy. Prior retrospectives already warned that top-level epic rows can remain `in-progress` while all stories and retrospectives are complete.

## Key Lessons

1. A deferred-work ledger needs executable invariants, not just careful prose. Row IDs, current-state markers, and owner counts made the work reviewable.
2. Accepted constraints need the same rigor as fixes. Owner, risk, downstream impact, evidence, and reopen trigger are what make an acceptance defensible.
3. Broad sweep stories need row-level closure mechanics from the start. Story 11.5 shows how easy it is for summary-level closure to diverge from detailed row markers.
4. Release readiness is a state of evidence, not a story status. Tests passing is necessary, but release confidence also needs provider, credential, stakeholder, and manual-verification evidence where those are part of the contract.
5. Review loops work best when they produce small, named patches. The strongest improvements came from decision-needed and patch findings that were resolved directly in the story records.
